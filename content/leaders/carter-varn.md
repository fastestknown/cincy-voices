# Carter Varn — Leader Profile Reference

## Role & Identity
**Fractional CIO / IT Leadership** | Technology Strategy, Cybersecurity, Vendor Management
Works primarily with established companies — manufacturers, commercial construction trades — that have been around for decades but whose technology hasn't kept up with their ambition.

---

## The Origin Story

Carter's work ethic was forged at 15, working in his father's trucking company garage alongside truckers and mechanics.

> "My dad ran a company, a trucking company, for 35 years, and early on at 15, I was thrown in the garage with the truckers and the mechanics and was taught to work hard and get through some tough spots."

His father remains his biggest influence:

> "He ran a trucking company for 35 years and I saw all that he could bring from a leadership perspective, and he was always in my corner early on as I was endeavoring and figuring out what I was going to do next in life."

---

## What Makes Carter Remarkable

### 1. He Works Himself Out of a Job — On Purpose
Carter's signature move is building an organization's IT capability to the point where they can bring in full-time leadership. He doesn't cling to the engagement — he celebrates the graduation.

His defining client story: A second-generation manufacturer that had never had anyone in IT leadership. Still running on 10-year-old technology. Carter came in, fixed vendor relationships, stood up a cybersecurity program, put them on the right path with data and business intelligence, and then **helped them hire a full-time Director of IT** — managing the interviews and onboarding just like a full-time employee would.

> "I came in and worked with them for a little over a year, fixed a lot of the relationships with their outside vendors, pushed them in the right direction and kind of steered the ship and ultimately helped them hire a full-time director of IT."

### 2. He's a Fractional, Not a Consultant
Carter draws a hard line between the two:

> "Consultant, in my opinion, usually comes in, kind of shows you what you're going to do, gives you a plan maybe, and walks away. For me, I'm on those weekly leadership calls. I want that seat at the table."

He does strategy, planning, AND implementation — managing vendors, implementing software, bringing in outside resources. Full-time mindset on a part-time basis.

### 3. He Gets Quick Wins Because Tech Is Always Neglected
Technology is often the "redheaded stepchild" — left in a corner, unattended. Carter walks in and solves problems that have been festering for years.

> "The success comes pretty quick because typically technology is the redheaded stepchild that's left in the corner, left unattended. And I come in and can get some of those quick wins. 'We've been talking about this for 6 years and have never been able to get over the hump on this problem. And you were able to solve that relatively quickly.'"

### 4. He Figured Out Sales as a Non-Sales Person
Carter doesn't come from sales — he comes from technology and business. Getting clients was a challenge he had to deliberately solve.

> "I come from the world of technology and business, so I'm not natively a salesperson — so having to figure out how you're going to get those clients has been an interesting phenomenon early on. So I decided to really dive deep into networking and make that work to my advantage."

---

## Philosophy & Approach

- **Extension of leadership:** Views fractional as filling C-level seats on a part-time basis, not providing advice from the outside
- **Client readiness is key:** "If they're not ready for change, this isn't the way to go."
- **Two-for-one value:** The money spent on one FTE could solve problems in two areas through fractional — marketing and technology at the same time
- **Future of fractional:** Believes it started with marketing and CFO roles but is expanding rapidly into sales, technology, cybersecurity, and more
