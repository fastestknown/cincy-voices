Kevin Lawson does not have the problem most fractional leaders are trying to solve.

He is full.

Every business Kevin works with comes to him with a sales problem. That part is clear. But the more interesting thing about Kevin is not that companies need his help. It is what he does once he is already busy.

He keeps building around the work.

Kevin is a fractional sales leader who helps small businesses build the kind of sales systems they usually cannot afford to build on their own. He works with companies that are under-resourced, under-systemized, and trying to compete against larger organizations with more people, more capital, and more structure.

That is not an abstract market for him. It is personal.

Kevin grew up watching his father run a small business as an importer with an exclusive U.S. distributorship. He saw how much judgment, improvisation, and pressure sits inside a business that looks simple from the outside. Later, he worked in Fortune 500 construction wholesale distribution, then inside a family-owned company navigating a generational transition, then in software selling ERP into small and mid-sized businesses.

The pattern kept repeating.

Small businesses were trying to punch above their weight. They had real opportunity, real customer relationships, and real ambition. What they often lacked was a system.

That became Kevin's lane.

Not sales as pressure. Sales as structure.

Not change as a mandate. Change as something people can actually adopt.

One of Kevin's clearest beliefs is that empathy has to come before process. If a team is already operating in chaos, dropping a polished system on top of them does not create trust. It often creates more chaos.

So Kevin starts differently. He listens first. He studies the way work is actually happening. He looks for the controls that are missing, the handoffs that are unclear, and the moments where the owner is still carrying too much of the sales function personally.

Then he helps the company build a system people can live inside.

That is why one of his lines has stayed with me:

"I was not a hatchet man, I was a process guy."

There is a lot packed into that sentence.

Kevin is not showing up to cut people out of the business. He is showing up to make the business easier to operate. He is especially good at one of the hardest transitions in a growing company: the moment when the owner can no longer be the only real salesperson.

That transition is delicate. The owner knows the customer. The owner knows the promises that can and cannot be made. The owner often has years of trust built into every sales conversation. Replacing that with a role, a process, and a new person is not just an org chart change.

It is a trust transfer.

Kevin understands that. He helps companies codify what works without draining the life out of it.

He also refuses to treat sales as a separate machine. Kevin sees the whole enterprise: marketing, delivery, accounting, operations, and the customer experience after the deal is signed. Sales is not just pipeline and close rate. It is one function inside a living business.

That is part of what makes him valuable as a fractional leader. He wears the sales hat, but he does not think in a silo.

He has a phrase for the way he wants to show up:

"I'm accessible but not interim."

That is a good description of strong fractional leadership. The best fractional leaders are not temporary placeholders. They are real leaders who create clarity, transfer capability, and leave the business stronger than they found it.

Kevin is doing that with clients. But he is also doing something beyond his own client work.

He is investing in the community around the work.

Along with Sean O'Shaughnessey and others, Kevin has been helping create places where sales leaders and fractional leaders can connect, compare notes, share challenges, and grow together. That matters because most business problems do not stay neatly inside one function.

A sales issue may really be a marketing issue. Or an operations issue. Or a technology issue. Or a leadership issue.

When one of Kevin's clients needs help beyond sales, he does not want to search a directory and hope. He wants to know who he trusts. Who has been in the room. Who thinks clearly. Who will care about the client after the introduction is made.

That is the deeper story here.

Kevin is not just building a practice. He is building a bench.

For business owners, that changes the value of working with someone like him. You are not only getting one leader's expertise. You are getting access to the judgment, relationships, and care that leader has built around them.

For fractional leaders, Kevin's story is a reminder that a full calendar is not the same thing as full impact. At some point, the work becomes bigger than staying busy.

Kevin is an avid runner. He loves barbecue. He is devoted to his family, especially as his kids get older and move toward leaving the nest. There is something fitting about that. He is building a life where work fits around what matters most, and he is building a professional community that works the same way.

Less extraction. More trust.

Less solo hero work. More shared bench.

That is why Kevin is the right first editorial spotlight for this next chapter of Cincy Voices.

He represents the kind of leader Work With Meaning exists to support: generous, systems-minded, deeply relational, and committed to helping small businesses and fractional leaders grow in a way that still feels human.

That is what this whole project is about.

Not just finding the next fractional leader.

Getting to know the people you would actually trust.
